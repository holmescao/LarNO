Abstract
16
In urban areas, real-time early warning systems are used to mitigate the severe impacts of pluvial
17
flooding. Although such systems have witnessed much recent development through the use of deep learn-
18
ing methods, urban-scale high-resolution modeling remains fundamentally constrained by the massive
19
GPU memory demand for training neural networks. To overcome this bottleneck, we present LarNO, a
20
memory-efficient, discretization-invariant neural operator that learns continuous-space hydrodynamic
21
mappings to predict the spatiotemporal distributions of water depth based on dynamic rainfall and
22
static topographic and drainage conditions. This approach enables zero-shot generalization to high
23
resolution when trained solely on low-resolution data. Additionally, existing autoregressive neural oper-
24
ators compress complex dynamics into a single physical variable, leading to an information bottleneck
25
in modeling complex nonlinear spatiotemporal dependencies. To resolve this, we embed spatiotem-
26
poral feature extractors into a neural operator to implement latent autoregression. Theoretically, we
27
prove that our approach universally approximates continuous operators and enables zero-shot super-
28
resolution generalization. An empirical case study of a highly urbanized region spanning nearly 100 km2
29
in Shenzhen, China, subjected to observed rainfall events with spatiotemporal heterogeneity, demon-
30
strates that LarNO achieves large-scale, high-resolution (5 m, 5 min), long-duration (6 h) urban flood
31
real-time spatiotemporal forecasting to mm-level depth accuracy, reducing errors by more than half
32
compared to state-of-the-art neural operators, while delivering two orders of magnitude faster inference
33
speedup over a traditional hydrodynamic model. Besides, results from the ablation study validate the
34
effectiveness of latent autoregression. Moreover, LarNO supports few-shot transfer to unseen catch-
35
ments via fine-tuning. Furthermore, comprehensive parameter sensitivity analyses demonstrate the
36

robustness and effectiveness of LarNO. Our work provides a groundbreaking framework for real-time
37
early warning and refined management of large-scale urban flood events.

1 Introduction
41
Floods are natural disasters that occur with high frequency worldwide and can reach very large magnitudes.
42
During the first quarter of the 21st century, flood disasters have affected several billion people and caused over a
43
trillion dollars in economic losses (Centre for Research on the Epidemiology of Disasters, 2020; Liu et al., 2024).
44
Intensification of climate change-induced extreme rainfall (Prein et al., 2017) and the expansion of impervious
45
surface cover through rapid urbanization (United Nations and Social Affairs, 2018; Rentschler et al., 2023) have
46
significantly increased human exposure to flood risk. Alongside structural interventions such as multistage flood
47
control channels (Mananoma et al., 2025) and nature-based water infrastructure (Supratman et al., 2025), real-
48
time flood early warning systems are well-established as critical non-structural tools for mitigating economic
49
losses (Pilon, 2004; Rogers and Tsirkunov, 2010) and saving lives (World Meteorological Organization, 2013; World
50
Health Organization, 2014). In highly urbanized catchments, the complex micro-terrain (Logan and Reilly, 2023),
51
high density of buildings (Bates, 2023), and intricate drainage system (Cao et al., 2025) result in strongly localized
52
flood dynamics, whose simulation requires the use of flood models of high spatial resolution of O(m) (Cao et al.,
53
2025). At the urban scale, flood models also require high temporal resolution of O(min) to adequately simulate
54
rapid inundation processes driven by extreme rainfall events with spatiotemporal heterogeneity (Zhang et al.,
55
2023). Hence, a model for large-scale, high-resolution spatiotemporal forecasting of urban flooding is urgently
56
needed to advance flood early warning systems and refine management decisions.
57
Traditional hydrodynamic models, such as SWMM (Huber et al., 1988), LISFLOOD-FP (Bates and De Roo,
58
2000), MIKE (Patro et al., 2009), and InfoWorks ICM (Cheng et al., 2017) provide accurate simulations of urban
59
flood dynamic processes. However, high-resolution numerical simulations (Chippada et al., 1998) are computa-
60
tionally intensive, requiring several hours to run on large-scale spatiotemporal domains due to convergence and
61
stability requirements, such as spatial and temporal resolutions of 5 m and 0.2 s, when solving the governing
62
shallow water equations (De Moura and Kubrusly, 2013). Despite advances in high-performance computing (Neal
63
et al., 2009; Sanders and Schubert, 2019; Schubert et al., 2022) and acceleration by graphics processing units
64
(GPU) (Gong et al., 2020; Ming et al., 2020; Buttinger-Kreuzhuber et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), traditional
65
hydrodynamic models still struggle to provide real-time urban flood spatiotemporal forecasts.
66
In recent years, deep learning methods have been increasingly applied to the spatiotemporal forecasting of
67
urban floods. Such methods learn underlying physical laws in an end-to-end data-driven manner (LeCun et al.,
68
2015), supporting large time steps (e.g., 5 min) at high spatial resolution and achieving computational speeds 2-3
69
orders of magnitude faster than traditional hydrodynamic models (Cao et al., 2025). The mainstream deep learning
70
2


=== Page 3 ===
architecture for urban flood spatiotemporal modeling involves coupling spatial networks (e.g., convolutional neural
71
networks, CNNs) with temporal networks (e.g., long short-term memory networks, LSTMs) to capture local
72
spatial features such as terrain and drainage system, while also modeling the temporal dependencies of flood
73
dynamics (Bentivoglio et al., 2023; Burrichter et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; Situ et al., 2024; Berkhahn and
74
Neuweiler, 2024; Nearing et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2025; Rau et al., 2025; Woo et al., 2025). However, large-scale,
75
high-resolution spatiotemporal modeling, involving millions of spatial mesh cells and sub-billions of spatiotemporal
76
points, demands a massive memory requirement (i.e., GPU memory) for neural network training that greatly
77
exceeds the GPU memory limit (Cao et al., 2025). Existing studies compromise by either using low spatial
78
resolution (e.g., 30 m (Rau et al., 2025)) and low temporal resolution (e.g., 60 min (Woo et al., 2025)), or limiting
79
the study to small areas (e.g., 1 km2) (Cao et al., 2025)), as shown in Fig. 1. Hence, large-scale, high-resolution
80
spatiotemporal modeling of urban floods is a bottleneck for deep learning methods.

This limitation arises because such deep learning methods are essentially discretization-dependent operators
82
that learn mesh mappings at a fixed resolution (Li et al., 2020). As a consequence, such methods are unable
83
to generalize to higher resolutions without training on high-resolution data (He et al., 2023). By contrast, neu-
84
ral operators are discretization-invariant neural networks that map inputs to solutions of partial differential
85
equations (PDEs) in continuous space (Kovachki et al., 2023). The discretization-invariant property enables neu-
86
ral operators to be trained on low-resolution data and then directly generalized to higher resolutions (zero-shot
87
3


=== Page 4 ===
super-resolution generalization) (Kovachki et al., 2023). Unlike conventional train–test settings where the reso-
88
lution must match, zero-shot super-resolution enables memory-efficient training. Although neural operators have
89
been applied to approximate flood dynamics (Xu et al., 2024, 2025), urban-scale high-resolution spatiotemporal
90
forecasting remains unexplored. Moreover, existing neural operators (Li et al., 2021; Rahman et al., 2022) face a
91
core challenge in spatiotemporal modeling. By employing an explicit autoregressive scheme (Gregor et al., 2014),
92
where predicted physical states are fed back into the model for step-by-step forecasting, the network is forced
93
to continuously compress complex system dynamics into a single physical variable. This information bottleneck
94
not only restricts the modeling of complex nonlinear spatiotemporal dependencies but also directly propagates
95
prediction errors, leading to rapid error compounding over long forecast horizons.
96
Here, we propose a novel neural operator, LarNO (pronounced “Lar-no”), that enables zero-shot generalization
97
to high resolutions, thereby achieving memory-efficient training for urban flood dynamics. LarNO is a latent
98
autoregressive neural operator that embeds spatiotemporal feature extractors for modeling complex nonlinear
99
spatiotemporal dependencies. We theoretically prove that LarNO has universal approximation capabilities for
100
continuous operators (such as the hydrodynamic operator) (Theorem 1), and enables zero-shot super-resolution
101
generalization (Theorem 2). To the best of our knowledge, LarNO is the first method to achieve modeling of
102
millions of spatial mesh cells and sub-billions of spatiotemporal points, surpassing existing studies by one order
103
of magnitude. This breakthrough overcomes the bottleneck in large-scale (≈100 km2), high-resolution (5 m, 5
104
min), long-duration (6 h) spatiotemporal forecasting of urban floods. Additionally, LarNO achieves mm-level
105
depth accuracy by using hydrodynamic simulation results as a reference and reduces errors by more than half
106
compared with existing neural operators, accompanied by a ∼940× acceleration in inference speed compared to
107
the reference hydrodynamic model. Furthermore, we demonstrate that few-shot fine-tuning effectively transfers
108
LarNO to a new catchment.

2 Methodology

2.1 Governing equations and hydrodynamic operator for urban flood dynamics
116
The spatiotemporal evolution of an urban flood can be modeled as a complex hydrodynamic system that couples
117
the 1D Saint-Venant equations (SVE) for the drainage system with the 2D shallow water equations (SWE) for
118
surface flow (Bates et al., 2010). The evolution process can be described as a continuous, nonlinear mapping in a
119
function space, where the input space includes source/sink terms, boundary conditions, and historical hydrody-
120
namic states, and the output space represents the hydrodynamic function. Therefore, the system can be generally

3 Theory
218
This section provides a theoretical analysis of LarNO, focusing on its key properties and performance guarantees.

3.1 Universal approximation of continuous operators by LarNO
221
To support the use of LarNO as a neural operator for nonlinear continuous systems, such as encountered in urban
222
flood dynamics, we use theoretical analysis to show that LarNO can universally approximate continuous operators.
223
Theorem 1 (Universal approximation of continuous operators by LarNO). Let D ⊂Rp, D′ ⊂Rq be compact
224
domains. Let the input function space be A = A(D; Rdin) and the output function space be U = U(D′; Rdout), both
225
assumed to be separable Banach spaces. Let NON(σ; A, U) denote the class of neural operators of depth N with
226
activation σ. Then, for any continuous operator G ∈C(A; U) and ∀ε > 0, there exists a LarNO Gθ ∈NON(σ; A, U)
227
such that
228
∥G(a) −Gθ(a)∥U < ε,
∀a ∈A.
(14)
Theorem 1 provides a solid basis for using LarNO in nonlinear dynamic systems. In the context of urban flood
229
modeling, Theorem 1 ensures that the mapping from external inputs to hydrodynamic responses can be learned
230
with guaranteed accuracy.
231
3.2 Zero-shot super-resolution generalization of LarNO
232
To support the use of LarNO in zero-shot super-resolution generalization, we provide a theoretical analysis of
233
error bounds for different discretization resolutions.
234
Theorem 2 (Discretization invariance of LarNO). Assume that LarNO Gθ (Eq. 9) satisfies the universal approxi-
235
mation property as stated in Theorem 1. Let a ∈A be input and aZn ∈PZnA be its projection onto a grid Zn ⊂D
236
with n grid points. Suppose the activation functions σ are Lipschitz continuous (Armijo, 1966), and each T i is a
237
Lipschitz continuous finite-dimensional map. Then, for ∀ε > 0, there exists Np ∈N such that for all n > Np,
238
∥Gθ(aZn) −Gθ(a)∥U < ε.
(15)
Theorem 2 proves that LarNO is discretization-invariant, meaning the error bounds remain stable across
239
different discretized resolutions, thereby ensuring the validity of the zero-shot super-resolution generalization.
240
Note that this only holds when the discretization is sufficiently fine (i.e., a sufficiently large number of grid points
241
n) to approximate the continuous space of the nonlinear dynamical system (i.e., to resolve adequately the key
242
features of the system). In practice, the required resolution depends on the heterogeneity of the target system,
243
and the appropriate discretization level should be determined empirically.

dynamics of representative urban flood events induced by spatially heterogeneous extreme rainfall. First, we
251
utilized urban geographic information at 5-m spatial resolution to resolve street-level flood modeling. Then, we
252
collected 80 observed extreme rainfall events previously recorded in the study region between 2008 and 2018, with
253
return periods ranging from 0.1 to 20 years. All events were assigned a 6-hour duration to facilitate training and
254
evaluation. We randomly selected multiple rainfall centers to generate spatially heterogeneous extreme rainfall
255
events, typical of an urban region spanning 100 km2. A given center utilized an observed rainfall time series,
256
whereas the other centers generated new rainfall time series by perturbing the original series with 50% Gaussian
257
noise. For the remaining region, rainfall time series were generated using inverse distance weighting at 1 km
258
resolution.
259
To simulate pluvial urban flooding, we used MIKE Plus Version 2023, employing a 1D-2D coupled scheme.
260
Specifically, the 1D model represents the underground sewer networks and stormwater drainage pipes, while the
261
2D model captures the surface runoff. These two domains are dynamically coupled through drainage inlets, which
262
11


=== Page 12 ===
were configured according to available city planning data. We elevated the terrain values at building locations
263
by 100 m to simulate the impact of buildings on surface runoff and flood propagation. The model time step was
264
adjusted between 0.01 s and 1 s to maintain numerical stability (De Moura and Kubrusly, 2013). Regarding the
265
applicability conditions and reproducibility, the reference numerical model assumes a closed boundary across the
266
study domain and a dry bed initial condition. Model parameters (i.e., surface roughness) calibrated in previous
267
work (Cao et al., 2025) were used to simulate 6-hour spatiotemporal water depths at 5-minute intervals. These
268
parameters are considered valid for the current application, as they were calibrated and validated against historical
269
observation data in the same catchment, ensuring the reliability of the reference data.
270
Finally, we constructed a benchmark dataset for urban flooding, comprising 80 events with spatial dimensions
271
of 1,600×2,240 and a temporal dimension of 72. These events were randomly divided into a 4:1 training and
272
testing set to evaluate the performance of neural operators for unseen events.

4.2.2 Implementation details
285
Inputs to the neural operator include sequences of rainfall intensity, cumulative rainfall, Digital Surface Model
286
(DSM), imperviousness ratio, and drain inlet area. At each time step, the input rainfall intensity and cumulative
287
rainfall are based on values at the previous 6 time steps, reflecting the time of concentration in the water-
288
shed (Maidment, 1992). Specifically, the static spatial features (e.g., DSM and drain inlets) are concatenated with
289
the dynamic rainfall features at each time step, and the combined input is then fed into the lifting operator. All
290
input features are preprocessed using min-max normalization. The model outputs a 2D water depth map at each
291
prediction step. The present version of LarNO consists of 4 layers of neural networks, each with 32 channels, and
292
frequency domain modes set with 100 width and 140 height. To account for the relatively small proportion of wet
293

294

295
Ba, 2014) with warm-up (He et al., 2016) and cosine annealing (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2016) learning rate strate-
296
gies. During implementation, the learning rate is increased linearly from 0 to 10−2 over the first 10 epochs, using
297
a warm-up strategy, and then gradually decreased to 10−4 during the final epoch, following the cosine anneal-
298

299
A800 PCIe GPUs with 80 GB of memory. During the testing phase, we use an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with
300

4.3 Comparison with state-of-the-art neural operators for urban flood forecasting
305
We now compare results from LarNO against those using a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021) and a
306
U-shaped Neural Operator (UNO) (Rahman et al., 2022) in terms of predictive accuracy. FNO is a classic neural
307
operator that uses Fourier transforms and Multilayer Perceptrons to efficiently learn mappings between infinite-
308
dimensional function spaces. UNO is a variant of FNO, which incorporates the U-Net architecture. The methods
309
are tested for their skill in large-scale, high-resolution, long-duration spatiotemporal forecasting of urban floods in
310
the 100 km2 study area in Shenzhen, China. All neural operators were first optimized on a low-resolution (20 m)
311
training dataset to adjust network parameters. Then, a zero-shot super-resolution generalization was performed
312
on test sets at higher spatial resolutions (5 m, 10 m, and 15 m).
313

314

316
with the smallest absolute error in peak water depth (Fig. 5e). By contrast, FNO and UNO incur significant
317
spatial errors. Additionally, FNO tends to predict dry cells as wet cells, resulting in overly smoothed results with
318
inaccurate details. To check the accuracy of each method in long-duration forecasting, we calculated their values
319
of MAE, cumulative MAE, and CSI. As shown in Fig. 5f, LarNO accumulates error significantly more slowly,

with its MAE over a 6-hour lead time being less than half that of FNO. LarNO also achieves significantly higher

accuracy in identifying the flood distribution (with CSI>0.6 throughout the flood duration). Moreover, from the

325
operators in long-duration forecasting.

348

4.6 Parameter sensitivity analysis
414
This section examines the effects of several key hyperparameters on LarNO’s performance through comprehensive
415
sensitivity analyses, including the frequency-domain mode proportion, the number of neural network layers, the
416
number of channels per layer, the loss weighting coefficient (λ), and the spatial resolution of the training data.
417
First, we evaluate the impact of structural hyperparameters and the loss weighting coefficient on model accu-
418
racy and computational overhead (Fig. 11). As the frequency-domain mode proportion, number of NN layers,
419
and number of channels increase, both GPU memory demand and training time increase significantly (Fig. 11a,
420
c, e). However, the model’s zero-shot super-resolution accuracy stabilizes once the capacity reaches a mode pro-
421
portion of 0.25, 4 layers, and 32 channels (Fig. 11b, d, f). These performance thresholds can be attributed to
422
several underlying mechanisms. Regarding the Fourier modes, lower-frequency modes generally capture the pri-
423
mary large-scale spatial patterns of the flood dynamics. While incorporating higher-frequency modes can help
424
represent finer-grained local variations, the marginal improvement in predictive accuracy diminishes rapidly. For
425
the network depth, performance degrades when exceeding 4 layers, likely because excessive depth impedes effec-
426
tive gradient propagation during training (He et al., 2016). For the channel width, 32 is the best; smaller widths
427
exhibit insufficient representation capacity, whereas excessively large widths introduce redundant parameters
428
that lead to overfitting. Furthermore, evaluating the loss weighting coefficient λ reveals that the prediction error
429
reaches its minimum around λ = 20 (Fig. 11g, h). This specific weight effectively compensates for the spatial class
430
imbalance, properly penalizing errors in the relatively small proportion of flooded areas. These findings provide
431
strong empirical justification for our choices, as they achieve the optimal balance between predictive accuracy and
432
memory-efficient training.
433
Next, we train multiple versions of LarNO, FNO, and UNO using datasets with different spatial resolutions of
434
{15 m, 20 m, 40 m, 80 m, 100 m}. As shown in Fig. 12a,d,e, LarNO generalizes with high accuracy across resolutions
435
and significantly outperforms other methods. Additionally, in the zero-shot super-resolution generalization task,
436
performance improves as the trained spatial resolution increases, whereas model accuracy stabilizes when the
437
resolution reaches 20 m.
438
In short, to ensure accuracy and improve training efficiency with limited GPU memory (as shown in Figs. 11
439
and 12b-e), we set the frequency-domain mode proportion to 0.25, the number of layers to 4, the number of
440
channels to 32, λ to 20, and the spatial resolution of the training dataset to 20 m for all other experiments.

possesses this property (Theorem 1), but note that the resolution must be sufficiently fine to resolve key features,

The latent autoregressive scheme of LarNO is implemented by embedding a spatiotemporal feature extractor
457
(i.e., ConvGRU) into the Fourier layer. We also prove theoretically that this novel neural operator retains a uni-
458
versal approximation capability similar to that of conventional neural operators. Our ablation studies demonstrate
459
that incorporating latent autoregression substantially reduces both spatial and temporal errors over long forecast
460
horizons. This is because conventional explicit autoregression feeds predicted physical states back into the model,
461
forcing the network to compress complex dynamics into a single variable and directly propagating prediction
462
errors. In contrast, latent autoregression can model temporal dependencies via high-dimensional learnable hidden
463
states. Meanwhile, ConvGRU jointly extracts spatiotemporal features, utilizing gating mechanisms to adaptively
464
control information flow and capture long-term physical trends. By maintaining a more accurate representation of
465
the system’s dynamics, this approach improves step-wise prediction accuracy and naturally mitigates error com-
466
pounding, thereby addressing the shortcomings of explicit autoregression neural operators in modeling complex
467
nonlinear spatiotemporal dependencies (Cao et al., 2025). It should be noted that the comparisons with FNO
468

469
on data generated by the same numerical model under identical conditions. Operational hydrological forecasting
470
involves substantially greater challenges, including real-time data assimilation, uncertainty quantification under
471
observational noise, and generalization to diverse ungauged basins. The performance advantages demonstrated
472
here therefore reflect LarNO’s architectural improvements over existing neural operators within this benchmark
473
setting, and should not be interpreted as a direct measure of readiness for operational deployment.
474
Finally, LarNO shows strong potential for transfer learning. The model can quickly adapt to new catchments
475
using a small number of training samples. By fine-tuning the pre-trained weights, LarNO retains its scale-invariant
476
physical mappings, ensuring robust super-resolution performance.

5.2 Limitations and future work
478
Although the present study has made significant progress in urban flood spatiotemporal modeling, the following
479
critical challenges remain to be explored. (1) Application to general flood modeling is yet to be achieved. Urban
480
flooding differs from other types of flooding in terms of sources, sinks, and boundary conditions. This requires
481
neural network models to be able to adapt to a range of input variables flexibly. For example, to predict the
482
spatiotemporal dynamics of a storm surge (Yang et al., 2020) or river overflow flooding (Fraehr et al., 2023), LarNO
483
model requires tidal levels or river stages as inputs. (2) Zero-shot generalization to unseen regions. Most studies,
484
including LarNO, train neural networks on data from a single region and evaluate their generalization to different
485
rainfall events (He et al., 2023; Situ et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2025). However, applying these models to new regions
486
requires generating new samples to retrain or fine-tune the neural network (Xu et al., 2025). Although researchers
487
have achieved spatial generalization in breach flood modeling (Bentivoglio et al., 2023), urban flood modeling is
488
more complex because it involves the accurate simulation of underground drainage system dynamics. This remains
489
a key direction for our future work. (3) Historical flood event reconstruction under data scarcity. While data-
490
driven models thrive on massive datasets, direct flood observations are exceptionally scarce in both space and
491
time (Nearing et al., 2024; Soebroto et al., 2025). This scarcity severely limits the direct training of neural networks
492
on real-world events. Therefore, a critical future direction is to develop advanced architectures and few-shot
493
training methodologies capable of robustly reconstructing historical flood dynamics from highly sparse and limited
494
observational data. (4) The inherent limitations of surrogate modeling. Because LarNO is trained to approximate
495
the solutions of MIKE+ rather than true “real-world” physics, its performance is inherently bounded by the
496
reference model’s fidelity. Consequently, any structural errors, parameter uncertainties, or simplified physical
497
assumptions inherent to the MIKE+ simulations are inevitably baked into the neural operator’s “ground truth.” In
498
the broader and more challenging context of operational hydrological forecasting, safely deploying such surrogate
499
models will require integrating real-world observational data through data assimilation or by incorporating physics-
500
informed constraints to actively mitigate these inherited biases.

6 Conclusion
502
This paper proposed neural operators for memory-efficient training, which achieve fast, accurate urban flood fore-
503
casts at large-scale and high-resolution. Our model, called LarNO, embedded spatiotemporal feature extractors
504
into the neural operator to implement latent autoregression, enhancing the representation of complex nonlinear
505
spatiotemporal dependencies. Theoretical analysis and empirical results demonstrated the effectiveness and supe-
506
riority of LarNO, whose inundation depth predictions were O(mm) accurate compared to the reference solution
507
generated by an advanced hydrodynamic model for a flood in a district of Shenzhen, China; this level of accuracy
508
is far better than achieved by state-of-the-art neural operators, and is accompanied by two orders of magnitude
509
faster in inference time over the numerical model. Furthermore, transfer learning experiments confirm that LarNO
510
can efficiently adapt to unseen regions using limited data. In short, our method offers a novel solution for real-time
511
early-warning systems and local management of large-scale urban flood events.
512
In practice, our proposed approach can be integrated with expert-level rainfall forecasting techniques to support
513
urban disaster warning systems and help inform climate-adaptive policy decisions, such as encountered in the
514
design of drainage systems. Moreover, LarNO can be readily deployed in public-facing applications, enabling
515
residents to receive real-time flood warnings on their personal mobile devices. Such an approach would enhance
516
public awareness and engagement in disaster response, thereby strengthening overall urban resilience. Despite
517
present progress, further work is needed before LarNO can be widely deployed. Key future directions include
518
modeling compound flood drivers, such as river discharge and storm surges, improving generalization to unseen
519
regions, reconstructing flood events using limited historical observations, and addressing the uncertainties inherent
520
in surrogate modeling by integrating real-world physical constraints and observational data assimilation.
